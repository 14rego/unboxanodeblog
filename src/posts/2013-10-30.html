I’m trying to be better at saying I love something, only to throw it aside after a while. I try to use it for a few months, then decide if it continues to change the way I do things.

For the last year or so, I’ve been playing with different coding IDEs. I read blogs, I asked around on forums, I talked to my friends, I tried at least six different ones for several weeks. Do you know how many options there are? Everyone thinks that theirs is “the real deal” and no one should hear otherwise.

Based on the fact that I’d been using the Adobe Creative Suite for years, I have always preferred Dreamweaver. But I knew that was the designer in me. Having all of my software work together seamlessly was just too good to question. Until I started using GIT.

This made me question everything. I knew there were other IDEs out there that had version control systems and I was bound to find one. But every time I tried something new and got the hang of it and set up all my FTP and repo settings, I always found something Dreamweaver had that I thought I couldn’t live without.

I know you think you knew where I’m going with this. I found my perfect IDE with all my desired solutions (GIT, FTP sync, powerful find and replace, code-hinting, code-linting, etc.) wrapped up in one little ball? I didn’t. It doesn’t exist for someone like me.

I did, however, find a great solution. It wasn’t free, unfortunately, but it’s not expensive like Dreamweaver. Thanks to my previous years of print designing, I’ve always upgraded to the latest Creative Suite and just enjoyed Dreamweaver because of it. But those days are over.

Now, I use Espresso for Mac. It does everything I need except manage my repos, but I have Sourcetree for that and no one does it better. These two tools have become my rocks. I “never leave home without it.”
